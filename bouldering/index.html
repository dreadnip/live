Bouldering cheatsheet

This page is my personal, subjective collection of bouldering related information.

Basic technique

When possible, climb with stretched arms. Our legs are generally stronger than our arms so they're more efficient to generate power with.

To get your body in a position where upwards movement can be built from your legs, use your core to create body tension and rotate into the desired position.

Balance

To stay balanced on the wall, you usually want to aim for (at least)2 opposite points of contact. E.g. right hand + left foot.

If you can, establish 3 points. This will almost always result in a stable position.

When climbing a route try to move from one stable position to the next.

Twistlock

To make reaching far away holds easier, we turn our hip into the wall as we move. Which side depends on which hand you're reaching with. Right hand = right hip, left hand = left hip.

Don't pull yourself into this position, hold it and then reach upwards to the hold. You actually want to twist in and reach up in one fluid motion.

Footwork

Precise footwork is important and often neglected. Here's some basic pointers.

  • Look at your feet when moving/placing.
  • Either use your tip, so you can pivot on it, or the inside edge on slabs.
  • Try to aim for slow, controlled movement instead of rapid bursts.
  • Don't bounce your foot to test the hold. Place your foot with care, re-adjust if necessary and stick.
  • On volumes, place the entire foot down to get as much contact surface with the volume as possible, as opposed to using your tip.
  • Try not to overshoot the hold. Don't place your foot above the hold and slide down onto it. Try to be as accurate as possible.

Flagging

To prevent swinging to the side and to keep your center of gravity above the hold you're standing on, you can stretch the leg that isn't supporting you outwards and press it against the wall.

Don't forget to actively press with the foot you're flagging.

Once you get the hang of flagging, you can also do back flag or inside flag to make up for not having 2 opposite points of contact.

Which side you flag to, and with which leg, depends entirely on your body position and the position of the available footholds.

Route reading

This is an essential skill. Being able to determine the moves and their sequence are key to climbing a boulder.

Hold direction

The basis of route reading is determining hold direction. To use a hold as efficiently as possible, you have to load your weight perpendicular to the edge that you're going to grab.

Most holds have an obvious way you should be holding them, which tells you a lot about how your body should be positioned once you reach that part of the problem.

Good video on directional loading

Visualizing the sequence

A more advanced version includes visualizing the entire sequence to a problem in your head. Doing this takes practice; doing just the hands is a good starting point.

When working out a sequence, don't stop once you figure out the first couple of holds. The end is usually the hardest because by then you're already exhausted so definitely take the extra time to map everything out, all the way to the top.

Measure yourself in holes by standing next to the wall. Now you have an idea of your reach when trying to visualize yourself climbing a route.

Dynamic moves

Keep hands on the start holds as long as possible. Power comes from the legs. Keep toes on as long as possible. Move in one fluid motion, establishing kinetic energy all the way from your toes up through your body. Move arms sideways after letting go of the starting hand holds. Once you land the move, pull yourself up to minimize momentum, then release back to stretched arms in a slow, controlled way.

Warm-up

Start with basic cardio to get your heart rate up. Either bike/run to the gym or do a combination of the following to get warm once you get there.

  • 10 push-ups
  • 10 squats
  • 10 high steps
  • 10 burpees
  • 10 jumping jacks
  • 10 mountain climbers

Once you feel warm, loosen up by doing some dynamic stretches:

  • arm circles
  • horizontal cross-over arm swings
  • torso spins
  • leg swings (frontal + sideways)
  • sideway lunges

Finally, start with a first set of easy routes. Climb 3-5 very easy routes both up and down.

These easy routes are a great time to focus on technique. Some solid exercises to do are: ninja climbing, no-hands on easy slabs, hover hands, shoulder touch, no re-adjusting, ...

Climbing drills

All of the following exercises should be done on practice climbs. These are usually 2 levels under your max level but can be even lower. Start easy and go up a level if they're too easy.

Technique

  • Limb elimination Climb a route 4 times in a row with 30s rest in between. Eliminate a different limb every time.
  • Eliminator Climb a route, eliminate a hold after each set until you can't finish it anymore.
  • Hover Climb a route. Before grabbing the next handhold, hover over it for 3 seconds.
  • Shoulder touch Every time you grab a new handhold, pull in your shoulder on that side and touch the hold with it.
  • Nose 2 hold You can only use a handhold once you've touched it with your nose.
  • Ninja climbing Climb without making a sound with your feet. Do 5 push-ups for each time you bump into the wall/hold and make a sound.
  • Colorblind Climb a route using the handholds of that route, but without using the footholds of the same color. Instead, you can use any other footholds on the wall.

(Power) Endurance

  • 4x4 Climb a route 4 times in a row with no rest in between. Take 2 minutes rest after the set, then do it again on 3 other routes. If too easy, go up a level rather than skipping resting.
  • Traversing Climb on any colour or on a spray wall. Focus on technique (silent feet, stretched arms, body position, flagging, ...). Start with 1 min on, 4 minutes rest. Do 6 sets of this.
  • Stick Have one person traverse and the other one build the route as they climb. Use a brush/stick/laser for pointing. Can also be used for power with short, powerful boulder (4-5 moves).
  • Cut loose After every hand move, make your feet cut loose, move into a full hang and then pull your feet back on.
  • Perfect repeats 4x a route. Find an imperfection in your climbing after each time. Correct it for the next repeat.
  • Beta repeats Find a 4-5 different betas for the same boulder.

Power

  • Contact strength Jump to a maximum effort hold (no jugs, max distance) on a training/spray wall and hold the position for 3-5 seconds. If you can do the move on the first try, pick a harder move.
  • Explosive pull-ups 3x3. Go as high as possible, as fast as possible
  • Short powerful boulders max 5-6 moves, steep/overhanging terrain

Training

These exercices can be done either at the end of a climbing session or on days when you can't get to the gym.

Each list is 1 set. You can do both, mix or just pick one. Aim for 3 to 5 sets depending on level. 1 minute rest between each exercice.

Finger strength

Just boulder really hard + max hangs. Find limit boulder moves and give it 4 max effort tries with good rest in between.

Power

Campus, explosive pull-ups, weighted (max) pull ups, pistol squats, weighted squats, double hand jumps

Strength

Lock off's, one arm lock off progression, one arm pull up progression

Core

  • Flutter kicks
  • Mountain climbers
  • Planks (front & sides)
  • Crunches
  • Superman
  • Leg stretches, with elbow support
  • Leg raises
  • Russian twist
  • Toe touches
  • L-sit
  • Front lever progression

Antagonist

  • Push ups
  • Dips
  • Rows

All numbers can be increased. Variations can be used to increase difficulty but quality > quantity.

Stretching

Stretching is essential to perform well on the wall. It prevents injuries, increases flexibility.

After every session, try to include a short cool-down of 10-15 minutes where you stretch your upper body and hips.

Rest days

Rule of thumb:

Avoid climbing 2 days in a row during your first year of climbing

When you start to climb you need to give your tendons and muscles enough time to adjust to the stress climbing puts on them. During the first year, you'll get stronger really quickly and you need to give your tendons time to adjust to this new load/power.

If you do cheat on this rule, listen to your body.

An example schedule:

Mon Tue Wed Thu Fri Sat Sun
Climb Rest Climb Rest Rest Climb Rest

A rest day can be either full on rest or doing some light cardio (walk/bike), light exercises and/or stretching.

Good article on rest days by Galina Parfenov.

Skin care

Use a pumice stone or 100-150 grit sandpaper to sand down the calluses that form on your hands to prevent flappers.

Wash off chalk right after a session and apply a lotion.

Keep nails at a decent, short length.

Tape isn't necessary for beginners.
